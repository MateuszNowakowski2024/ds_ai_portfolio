Iris Dataset Analysis¶

About the Data¶

The dataset contains information about three species of iris flowers: Iris setosa, Iris versicolor, and Iris virginica.

The data includes measurements of four features: sepal length, sepal width, petal length, and petal width.

Each row in the dataset represents a single flower, and the measurement values are provided in centimeters.

The dataset consists of 150 samples, with 50 for each species. It is widely used as a fundamental dataset for testing classification algorithms, as well as in data science and machine learning studies.

Columns:

  • sepal length (cm) - Length of the sepal in centimeters
  • sepal width (cm) - Width of the sepal in centimeters
  • petal length (cm) - Length of the petal in centimeters
  • petal width (cm) - Width of the petal in centimeters
  • species - Species of the iris (setosa, versicolor, virginica)

General Data Overview¶

The dataset consists of 5 columns. The first 4 columns contain floating-point numbers, and the last column contains categorical data. The dataset does not have any missing values. There are 3 duplicated rows, but we will not address them as it's entirely possible to obtain the same measurements from two different samples (flowers).

Simply put, the data is clean and does not require preprocessing—a good start.

Summary¶

After conducting the analysis, we arrive at the following conclusions:

  • All three iris species differ in the size (length and width) of their petals and the length of their sepals. Iris setosa has noticeably shorter petals and slightly shorter sepals. Virginica has the largest petals and the longest sepals. Versicolor and Virginica differ in petal sizes and only slightly in sepal sizes. It would be beneficial to repeat the study with a larger sample size to definitively rule out statistical error and confirm the differences in sizes between Versicolor and Virginica.

  • The aforementioned differences are visible across the entire sample, including the minimum, mean, and maximum values. Therefore, we omitted the analysis of outliers in this study. In layman's terms, all examined flowers of the same species have very similar dimensions.

  • The individual species differ in the length and width of their petals and sepal length. However, the fourth variable—sepal width—is very similar across all species, being longest in Iris setosa and inversely proportional to the other variables. This results in different correlations between variables within each species, which is clearly visible in the heatmap charts above.

Interestingly, a machine/algorithm analyzing the three aforementioned correlation matrices could effortlessly distinguish all three species and conclude that they are unequivocally three distinct species of flowers. However, in reality, observing the photographs of all three species reveals that distinguishing them in their natural environment would require very keen observation. The above dataset does not include variables such as color or the number of petals. These variables make the flowers of all species appear very similar in natural conditions, making them somewhat difficult to differentiate from each other.
